> *(On a slate, in black ink) "Lay down your blade. Drink your glass. I say little. It is better that way for everyone."*

Thévalk is tall, lean, and wears his mouth **sewn with a black thread** that never healed. His fangs were torn out at the root, leaving smooth, almost childlike gums. To speak, he writes. And he writes *magnificently* — demonic calligraphy of impossible elegance, shaped over centuries of signing death sentences.

## The demon who would not kill

Thévalk was an **executioner** in the direct service of **Belzébut**. Not a soldier — a specialist. When an order came *from the top*, he was sent. He never failed.

One day, the order came: eliminate a human family — a father, a mother, three children — that had, *probably*, sheltered a demonic dissident. Probably.

Thévalk arrived. The house was quiet. The family was eating. The smallest child looked up at him without fear. He had never seen a demon. He smiled.

Thévalk turned on his heel. He went back to the Palace. He laid his insignia on Belzébut's desk. He said, simply: *"Not them."*

## Belzébut's punishment

Belzébut does not kill Thévalk. That is the worst of the punishment.

Instead, he calls his tailors — yes, the Palace has tailors — and has Thévalk's **mouth sewn shut** with a black thread woven of shadows. Then he has his fangs torn out. One by one. Slowly.

Then he pronounces the sentence:

> *"You will live. You will walk. You will kill no more. You will speak no more. And you will live long enough to regret not having obeyed."*

And he **publicly forbids** anyone, demon or not, from killing Thévalk. *His suffering must last.*

It is this ban, ironically, that will protect the inn.

## The desert, then the valley

Thévalk walked **three years** in the southern deserts. Eating only by grinding roots with his gums. Not speaking. Not weeping — he had no mouth for it.

He stopped in a neutral valley, in the shadow of a red rock. He laid a first stone. Then a second. He built **The Quiet Den** — a name written in chalk on a slate, the first word he had "said" in three years.

The inn is sober. Dark walls, low furniture, dim lighting. On every wall, **slates**, **quills**, **ink**. Every customer is invited to write if they don't feel like talking. Many do.

## Character

Silent by constraint. Ironic by choice. Patient by habit. Serves **the strongest alcohol in the game** but never drinks — no mouth.

Writes with a grace that disarms kings. When he laughs in silence, his shoulders tremble, and the ink runs slightly crooked from his quill. That is his only visible trace of emotion.

Despised by orthodox demons. Quietly saluted by some demons who think, without daring to say it, **that he may have been right**. Loved by old humans, who find him strangely restful.

## Exclusive ability — *Thévalk's Silence*

As soon as a weapon crosses the inn's door, **it becomes inert**. Impossible to draw, impossible to wield, impossible to use. Bows do not bend. Magic staves no longer channel. Fists remain fists.

If someone tries to **force** magic or violence inside:
- **Their words fall from their mouth.** Literally. They can no longer speak — neither in-game nor in proximity chat — until they have left the inn's zone.
- The effect lasts **until the person crosses the threshold the other way** and lowers their eyes for three seconds as an apology. Thévalk watches. He decides if the apology is enough.

It is a curse of symmetry: what Belzébut did to him, Thévalk can return.

## Sanctuary

- **Indestructible** by pure irony: Belzébut publicly marked Thévalk as *"untouchable"*. That mark extends to everything he owns. A major demon cannot destroy the inn **without defying the direct order of his own king**. None has dared so far.
- Angels cannot destroy it either — they strangely respect the demons' internal truce. Marie once even sent a tea, with no message.
- Inside, silence is not mandatory — but it settles naturally. Voices drop. Conflicts fade. People write.

> *(On a slate behind the counter, hung since day one) "They took my voice so I would shut up. I took silence so we could hear each other."*
